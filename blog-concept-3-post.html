The Golden Hour in Ravello

Gregory Day Jan 15, 2025 · 8 min read

View from the Terrace of Infinity, Villa Cimbrone

There's a moment, roughly forty minutes before sunset, when the Amalfi Coast stops performing for tourists and begins its private show. The limestone cliffs catch fire. The sea turns from postcard-blue to molten copper. And Ravello — perched 350 meters above it all — becomes the best seat in the house.

I've photographed this coast for over a decade. Shot it in rain, in scorching August heat, in the eerie quiet of January. But nothing compares to Ravello's golden hour. It's the reason I moved here, and it's the reason I keep reaching for my camera even when I tell myself I'll just watch.

The Villa Cimbrone Terrace

The Terrace of Infinity at Villa Cimbrone is, without exaggeration, one of the most photographed spots on the Mediterranean. But here's the secret most visitors miss: the best light doesn't hit the terrace itself. It hits the bust-lined walkway approaching the terrace, about twenty minutes before the sun touches the horizon.

The bust-lined approach to the Terrace of Infinity — 5:47 PM, January

At this angle, the late sun creates long horizontal shadows across the stone path. The marble busts glow warm against the deep blue of the Tyrrhenian Sea behind them. It's a composition that practically shoots itself — if you know where to stand.

The difference between a good Ravello photo and an extraordinary one is about fifteen minutes and thirty meters of positioning.

Technical Notes

Ravello's golden hour demands a specific approach. The light changes faster than you'd expect at this altitude, and the contrast between the shadowed valley and the sunlit terraces can easily overwhelm a sensor.

Recommended Settings
Lens24-70mm f/2.8 or 35mm prime
Aperturef/5.6 – f/8 for landscapes
ISO100–400, bump as sun drops
White BalanceShade (5500–6500K) for warmth
FormatRAW always, bracket if needed

Best Times to Visit

The quality of Ravello's golden hour varies dramatically by season. Winter (December–February) offers the lowest sun angle, creating the most dramatic shadows and warmest tones. The trade-off is a shorter window — you'll have perhaps 25 minutes of peak light before the sun disappears behind the mountains.

Spring and autumn provide the best balance: longer golden hours, warm-but-not-harsh light, and manageable crowds. My personal favorite? Late October. The summer crowds have thinned. The gardens are still green but touched with the first gold of autumn. And the sunset light has that thick, amber quality that makes every frame look like it was shot through honey.
